			<a href="http://mrseb.co.uk/" rel="nofollow">MrSeb</a> writes <i>"The humble pixel &mdash; the 2D picture element that has formed the foundation of just about every kind of digital media for the last 50 years &mdash; may soon meet its maker. Believe it or not, if a team of British are to be believed, <a href="http://www.extremetech.com/extreme/143130-vector-vengeance-british-researchers-claim-they-can-kill-the-pixel-within-five-years">the pixel, within five short years, will be replaced with vectors</a>. If you know about computer graphics, or if you've ever edited or drawn an image on your computer, you know that there are two primary ways of storing image data: As a bitmap, or as vectors. A bitmap is quite simply a giant grid of pixels, with the arrangement and color of the pixels dictating what the image looks like. Vectors are an entirely different beast: In vector graphics, the image is described as a series of mathematical equations. To draw a bitmap shape you just color in a block of pixels; with vector graphics, you would describe the shape in terms of height, width, radius, and so on. At the moment, bitmaps are used almost exclusively in the realm of digital media &mdash; but that isn't to say they don't have their flaws. As display (and camera and cinema) resolution increases, so does the number of pixels. The obvious problem with this is that larger bitmaps are computationally more expensive to process, resulting in a slower (or more expensive) workflow. Pixel bitmaps don't scale very gracefully; reduction is okay, but enlargement is a no-no. There is always the issue of a master format, too: With pixel bitmaps, conversions from one format to another, or changing frame rates, is messy, lossy business. Which finally <a href="http://www.bath.ac.uk/news/2012/12/11/pixel-die/">leads us back to the innovation at hand</a>: Philip Willis and John Patterson of the University of Bath in England have <a href="http://eprints.gla.ac.uk/47879/1/ID47879.pdf">devised a video codec that replaces pixel bitmaps with vectors</a> (PDF)."</i></div>

			<div id="text-41080675" class="p">
			eldavojohn writes <i>"Dr. Charles 'Chip' Groat, lead author of <a href="http://www.oogeep.org/downloads/file/Publications/UT%20Study%20HF.pdf">a study claiming there was no link between fracking and water contamination</a>, has resigned at the University of Texas along with Dr. Raymond Orbach, the head of UT's Energy Institute.  The reason is that Groat served on the board of a drilling company and <a href="http://stateimpact.npr.org/texas/2012/12/06/review-of-ut-fracking-study-finds-failure-to-disclose-conflict-of-interest/">received compensation totaling over $1.5 million</a> from that entity over the last five years including time he spent writing the study.  After the Public Accountability Initiative gave the UT report a <a href="http://public-accountability.org/2012/08/ut-austin-chooses-oil-gas-industry-insider-to-review-fracking-report/">thorough beating for failing to mention this</a> it sparked UT to <a href="http://public-accountability.org/2012/12/university-of-texas-reviewers-blast-fracking-report-and-recommend-its-withdrawal/">recommend the report's withdrawal</a>.  PAI said the original report was 'based on literature surveys, incident reports and conjecture' and criticized UT's press from downplaying the many caveats.  PAI also said conclusions of the original report were 'tentative,' that the press coverage was 'inappropriately selective' and 'seemed to suggest that public concerns were without scientific basis and largely resulted from media bias.'  This study was <a href="http://news.slashdot.org/story/12/02/16/2336213/study-says-fracking-is-safe-in-theory-but-often-not-in-practice">also covered by Slashdot via MSNBC</a> quoting Groat and calling fracking safe in theory but not in practice."</i></div>
